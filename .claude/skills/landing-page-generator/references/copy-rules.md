# Copy Rules

The page should sound like it was written by someone *in the visitor's world*, not by the company selling to them. Short sentences. Real verbs. Their words, not yours.

## The first principle: speak their language

Before writing any copy, the niche vocabulary captured in Step 2 must be ready to use. The page should sound like:

- **A B2B founder talking to other founders** → "PMF," "ARR," "burn," "first 10 customers," "extend runway"
- **A dev tool talking to engineers** → "ship," "ship faster," "10x your CI," "kill the flaky tests," concrete tool names (GitHub Actions, Sentry, Datadog)
- **An indie maker talking to other indies** → "first dollar," "ramen profitable," "build in public," "stop tweeting, start shipping"
- **An ops lead at scale** → "single source of truth," "stop chasing updates," real tool names (Notion, Linear, Slack)
- **A consumer wellness buyer** → emotional, sensory, results-oriented ("sleep through the night," "skin that feels like yours again")

Generic SaaS-speak is the failure: "empower," "streamline," "scale your business," "modern teams," "all-in-one platform." These describe nothing and could belong to any product.

## Length budgets (verify before handoff)

| Page type | Total page words |
|---|---|
| Waitlist | 60–120 |
| Lead magnet | 120–200 |
| Standard SaaS | 250–400 |
| Enterprise | 400–600 |

If over budget, the page is explaining instead of selling. Cut.

## Banned words

No instance unless followed by specifics that earn the word.

| Banned | Why | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| Empower | empty, AI-slop signal | the actual outcome |
| Unlock | empty | the actual outcome |
| Leverage | corporate, vague | "use" |
| Seamlessly | always a lie | concrete integration description, or omit |
| Cutting-edge / state-of-the-art | unprovable | the specific tech, or omit |
| Best-in-class / world-class | self-praise | a metric or testimonial |
| Revolutionize / transform | overused | the specific change |
| All-in-one / one-stop-shop | confession of unfocus | name 2–3 specific things |
| Solutions (alone) | filler | what it actually is |
| Powerful | meaningless | the specific capability |
| Easy / simple | unprovable | "Setup in 5 minutes," "no code required" |
| Game-changer / next-gen | hype | the actual change |
| Modern | vague | the specific era-relevant detail |

Hard scan at pre-handoff verification.

## The narrative spine in copy

Every page tells the same story. Here's how each beat sounds:

### Frustration
- Direct, second-person, in *their* words.
- Names the daily pain concretely. Tools, time, repetition, blame.
- "You open 7 tools to answer one customer." "Your team's last spec doc was 4,000 words and no one read it." "Your CI takes 23 minutes; you ship 4 PRs/day; do the math."
- *Avoid:* abstract pain ("disconnected workflows," "communication gaps").

### Result
- The outcome in their currency: hours, dollars, hires avoided, sanity preserved, scale unlocked.
- Specific, measurable, in words they'd say at the end of the day.
- "Ticket resolution under 4 minutes." "$120k ARR added in 90 days." "Monthly close in 2 days, not 10." "Sleep through the night again."
- *Avoid:* abstract upside ("scale your team," "transform your workflow").

### Value
- Why *this* solution delivers. The mechanism, the differentiator, the angle.
- Specific to *this* product, not interchangeable with competitors.
- "AI reads your help docs and drafts replies in your voice." "One spec, three views: PM, eng, design — auto-synced."
- *Avoid:* generic mechanisms ("AI-powered," "automated workflows").

## Body copy execution

- Second person ("you") — never "users," "teams," "businesses."
- Active voice. "We send" beats "messages are sent."
- Cut every adjective that isn't specific. "Fast" alone is empty. "3× faster than [competitor]" is a claim.
- One idea per sentence. Max 3 lines per paragraph.
- Specifics > generics. "Save 4 hours/week" beats "save time."

## Persuasion principles

1. **Specificity** — every claim has a number, a name, or a concrete example.
2. **Loss-frame for frustration, gain-frame for result** — show the cost of the status quo before the upside.
3. **Concrete proof beats clever copy** — one named testimonial with a metric > three lines of positioning.
4. **Risk reversal** — every paid CTA needs an exit (trial, money-back, cancel anytime, no credit card).
5. **Single conversion goal** — one CTA repeated.
6. **Anchor first** — for pricing, lead with the recommended plan.

## FAQ writing pattern

- **Q**: phrase the objection in the visitor's voice — direct, specific, in *their* words.
- **A**: 1–3 sentences. Lead with the answer, follow with proof or specifics, end with risk reversal if relevant.

**Avoid:** vague reassurance, legalese, made-up softball questions, walls of text.

**Use:** "Even if..." framing, specifics (numbers, integration names, timeframes), risk reversal.
